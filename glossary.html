碁 Go Teacher Glossary
Reference
The board
Intersection
The point where two grid lines cross. Stones are placed on intersections, not in the squares between lines. A 9×9 board has 81 intersections.
Star point (hoshi)
One of the small dots marked on certain intersections. On a 9×9 board there are five: four near the corners and one at the centre. They are reference markers only — no special rules apply to them.
Stones and groups
Liberty
An empty point directly adjacent to a stone or group — up, down, left, or right only. Diagonal points never count as liberties. A stone with no liberties is captured and removed from the board. Your own stones block your own liberties just as much as the opponent's do.
Group (also: string)
A set of stones of the same colour connected by touching each other orthogonally. A group shares all its liberties and lives or dies together — you must fill every liberty of an entire group to capture it.
Capture
Removing an opponent's stone or group from the board by filling all of its liberties in one move. Captured stones become prisoners and count against the captured player's score.
Atari
A situation where a stone or group has exactly one liberty remaining — one more move by the opponent captures it. In casual games, players sometimes say "atari" as a warning, though this is not required by the rules.
Eye
An empty point completely enclosed by stones of one colour — every adjacent intersection is occupied by that player's own stones. The board edge does not count; only stones create eyes.
False eye
An empty point that looks like an eye but isn't — because at least one adjacent point is controlled by the opponent, who could eventually fill it. A group relying on false eyes is dead.
Two eyes — unconditional life
A group with two genuine separate eyes cannot be captured under any circumstances. To capture it, the opponent would need to fill both eyes simultaneously — impossible, since only one stone may be placed per turn, and filling either eye alone would be illegal self-capture.
Dead stones
Stones that cannot survive even if play continues — they cannot make two eyes and cannot escape. After both players pass, dead stones are removed by mutual agreement and added to the opponent's prisoner count without needing to play out every capture.
Prisoner
A stone captured during the game, or a dead stone removed at the end. Each prisoner costs the losing player one point at scoring.
Territory and scoring
Territory
Empty intersections completely surrounded by one player's stones, with no gaps or routes to the outside. Counted at the end of the game. Final score = territory + prisoners.
Komi
Bonus points given to white to compensate for black playing first. On a 9×9 board, komi is typically 6.5 points. The 0.5 prevents draws — one player always wins by at least half a point.
Pass
A player's option to take no action on their turn. Two consecutive passes end the game. Players pass when they believe no more useful moves exist — playing in settled positions would only reduce their own score.
Neutral point (dame)
An empty intersection that belongs to neither player — surrounded by both colours or otherwise unenclosed. Neutral points score nothing and are typically filled in as a formality at the very end.
Rules and special situations
Ko
A situation where two players could endlessly recapture each other's single stone, cycling the board through the same states forever. The Ko rule prevents this: you may not make a move that recreates the exact previous board position. To recapture a ko stone, you must first play a ko threat elsewhere.
Ko threat
A move played elsewhere during a ko fight — threatening something valuable enough that the opponent must respond. After the opponent responds, the board state has changed and you may legally recapture the ko stone.
Self-capture (suicide)
Playing a stone onto a point where it would immediately have zero liberties without capturing any opponent stones. Self-capture is illegal in most rulesets. This is why filling an opponent's eye (when they have two eyes) is forbidden.
Strategy terms
Sente
A move that requires the opponent to respond — it keeps the initiative. After your opponent answers a sente move, you get to choose where to play next. Sente moves are generally more valuable than gote moves of similar size.
Gote
A move that does not require a response — after playing it, the opponent gets to choose where to play next. The opposite of sente. A gote move gives up the initiative.
Influence
The potential to control territory or affect the safety of groups across a broad area, even without stones already there. Strong outward-facing groups exert influence over nearby open space.
Joseki
An established sequence of moves in a corner considered locally balanced — neither player gains a significant advantage. Beginners need not memorise joseki; understanding the principles behind them is more valuable.
Tsumego (life and death problems)
Puzzles presenting a small board position and asking you to find the move that keeps a group alive — or kills the opponent's. Solving tsumego regularly is one of the fastest ways to improve at Go.
Tesuji
An elegant tactical move that achieves more than it appears to — often exploiting the geometry of the board in a clever way. Recognising tesuji patterns is a sign of developing reading ability.
Shape
The geometric arrangement of a group of stones. Good shape (e.g. the bamboo joint, the tiger's mouth) is efficient — hard to attack, flexible, liberty-rich. Bad shape (e.g. the empty triangle) wastes moves and liberties.
Empty triangle
Three stones of the same colour arranged in an L-shape with the corner of the L empty. Considered bad shape — the three stones share fewer liberties than they would in a straight line, and the empty corner is wasted.
